I sometimes encounter a caricature of neurodiversity politics which presents it as basically a middle-class movement. In particular, a kind of politics of office workers, HR departments, and the more privileged university graduates. The implication seems to be that neurodiversity belongs to a world of professional-managerial anxieties, disconnected from ‘real’ working-class life. I’ll argue here that this is a highly misleading picture, though not a completely accidental one.

The first thing to note is that like many social movements under neoliberalism, neurodiversity advocacy often becomes most visible through people who have the resources, capital, education, and confidence to make themselves heard. Much of the mainstream discourse around neurodiversity-lite also tends to adopt the flattened language of liberal managerialism: inclusion workshops, awareness campaigns, corporate wellbeing initiatives. I often see this is neurodiversity academia and have consistently been critical of it. But importantly, this isn’t unique to neurodiversity. Most contemporary social movements struggle with the problem of what Olúfẹ́mi O. Táíwò calls [elite capture,](https://www.bostonreview.net/articles/olufemi-o-taiwo-identity-politics-and-elite-capture/) not to mention the broader marginalisation of class politics under neoliberalism. This must be resisted but it isn’t a good reason to dismiss the movements themselves.

Still, I do think there’s something worth analysing in the relative absence of blue-collar neurodiversity activism. Not because neurodivergent people are somehow absent from manual labour (obviously, we aren’t). But I do think most neurodiversity workplace advocacy has emerged in white collar contexts.

Before we get to this, it’s worth noting that part of the confusion in the critique of neurodiversity politics noted above, comes from how class itself is popularly understood on popular culturalist approaches. Despite decades of deindustrialisation, popular images of the ‘working class’ still tend to evoke images of manual labour: factories, warehouses, construction sites, tradesmen. City office workers, meanwhile, are often imagined as middle class almost by definition.

From a Marxian perspective, though, this distinction is mostly ideological. If you don’t own the means of production and have to sell your labour power in order to survive, you’re working class whether your labour is manual, emotional, or cognitive. The forklift driver and the academic are in largely the same relation to capital as long as they don’t own the means of production themselves.

At the same time, different forms of labour place very different demands on the nervous system. Consider one of the arguments I made in *[Empire of Normality](https://www.plutobooks.com/product/empire-of-normality/)*[:](https://www.plutobooks.com/product/empire-of-normality/) that the expansion of autism and ADHD diagnoses since the 1990s can partly be understood in relation to changes in labour itself. As economies shifted away from manufacturing and toward services, communication, and the attention economy, capitalism increasingly demanded new forms of self-regulation from workers. Emotional presentation, cognitive flexibility, attentional management, sociability all became more central to employability than they previously had been.

The contemporary office environment, for instance, is often particularly hostile. Open-plan offices, fluorescent lighting, constant interruptions, enforced sociability, and the expectation of continuous emotional performance all coalesce to produce new forms of neuronormative domination. Workers are expected not only to endure these sensory environments but, crucially, to remain socially responsive and emotionally legible within them for hours at a time. Neurodivergent disablement has thus expanded as more people compete for roles in environments are organised around continuous social and sensory performance.

In my own experience of many manual labour jobs, they often remain comparatively less restrictive in terms of neuronormativity. Not because they are more humane overall (they aren’t), but because they often place less emphasis on performative sociability and emotional self-management. When I worked in a sawmill, for instance, the environment was brutally loud and the physicality of the work was unforgiving. But everyone wore ear defenders and conversation was minimal. As someone with significant autistic sensory processing issues, I thus encountered far less difficulty as neurodivergent person there than I later did in academia. This was because nobody cared about eye contact, nobody expected performative enthusiasm, and nobody wanted me to constantly communicate my emotional state or affirm their obscure emotional needs.

Other manual jobs I worked in could even feel, from an autistic sensory perspective, unusually accommodating. Gardening and landscape gardening, at least whether the weather was nice, were sensory delights. The main sound was sometimes birdsong, and the light came from the sun. These roles also involved repetitive physical tasks outdoors for long periods of time, such as digging, lifting, and installing fence posts. There was a rhythm to the work that I found often calming rather than exhausting. Even though I was physically very tired at the end of the day, I didn’t experience burnout in the ways I regularly do in more white collar roles.

(Those are my experiences based around being autistic, but there could be many other examples. For instance, learning difficulties and intellectual disability may also be more accommodated in roles where you don’t need to write emails, update spreadsheets, calculate finances, and so on.)

To be sure, none of this is to romanticise manual labour, which comes with its own serious forms of exploitation, precarity, and bodily strain. These are well known and widely documented, and it is also worth noting that many neurodivergent people may be pushed towards low paid manual labour because of exclusion from other kinds of workplaces. Still, I do suspect that, on the whole, in terms of neurodivergent inclusion, many more traditional, now much rarer, blue-collar environments can be more accommodating than white-collar ones. A lot of people who might quickly require diagnostic recognition to survive in office environments simply pass relatively unnoticed in more traditional manual work settings because the social and cognitive norms are different. Thus, we can make sense of why more neurodiversity advocacy has come from white collar workers without dismissing neurodivergent activism in the ways I discussed at the start of the article.

That said, it would be wrong to think that neuronormativity is straightforwardly absent from manual labour. In some sectors it has simply taken another form, and is likely worsening every years. Amazon warehouses are perhaps the clearest example: manual workers subjected to algorithmic [surveillance](https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/business-68067022), constant productivity monitoring, and relentless optimisation of bodily and attentional energy. Here, the pressure is less about sociability and emotional presentation than about synchronising the worker’s body and attention to machinic systems of efficiency.

This is important to note as many of these workers, in so far as they are disabled, should technically have access to diagnosis and disability rights, leading to increased accommodations, free time, and so on. I don’t know how many have pursued this strategy, but I suspect many less than in more white collar settings. In so far as this is so, increased awareness of those possibilities will be an important tool for the fight to improve their work conditions, just as it has been in more white collar roles. As such, I think that increasing legibility of neurodivergence in manual labour outside the office or services role is vital, including through increased attention from unions and more organisations like [Red in the Spectrum](https://redinthespectrum.co.uk/).

If anything, as technologies of algorithmic neuronormativity and surveillance become increasingly powerful, the need to attend to this in blue collar setting will only become more pressing. In this sense, comrades, the struggle for neurodivergent class politics has a long way to go.
